> **TL;DR:** HITL is a design problem with three distinct timing regimes: design-time labeling, deploy-time validation, and runtime approval gates. Each has its own controls and its own failure modes. Regulators have different expectations of each. The frontline argument that modern runtime monitoring can substitute for deploy-time governance gets the trade wrong in a specific way: someone has to ask whether the runtime mechanisms themselves work under load, which is a deploy-time question. Multi-agent runtime HITL also faces structural problems that no checkpoint can solve on its own. Intermediate steps are opaque. Errors compound faster than humans respond. Automation bias drives systematic deference. Those problems compound when no deploy-time process has stress-tested whether the checkpoint will hold. Knight Capital's 2012 failure is the canonical illustration: runtime HITL in form, no deploy-time governance to validate it would function. The calibration question is how much HITL belongs at which timing regime, for which action classes. The emerging supervisory template is starting to name that question without yet resolving it.

The [previous post in this series](post.html?slug=hitl-vocabulary) established that "human-in-the-loop" is a borrowed term that banking regulators rarely use in their operative text. The [guardrails post](post.html?slug=guardrails) includes a four-tier risk table with a column labeled "HITL?" that takes Yes or No depending on the tier. The framing is wrong in a specific way. The conclusions are mostly right; the question being asked is wrong.

"Is HITL present?" is answerable with a checkbox. "Is HITL effective?" requires knowing when the human intervenes, what information they have when they do, what authority they hold to change the outcome, and whether they can intervene before an irreversible action commits. A Yes in the checkbox column and No to all four follow-on questions is the pattern that produced Knight Capital's $460 million loss in 45 minutes.

## The Three Timing Regimes of Human Oversight

Human oversight happens at three distinct points in an AI system's lifecycle, each involving different humans, different controls, and different regulatory expectations. Conflating them is how governance frameworks pass an audit while covering only one of three actual risk surfaces.

**Design-time HITL** is the labeling and preference-rating population behind RLHF and active learning, the humans whose judgments shape model behavior across all future deployments. That oversight is baked into the model before it touches a customer; it is not a substitute for an operator-level checkpoint at inference time.

**Deploy-time HITL** is MRM validation, conceptual soundness review, effective challenge, and red-team testing. This is where SR 11-7's apparatus lived. SR 11-7, superseded by SR 26-2 in April 2026, is where most of banking's human-oversight vocabulary was codified. SR 26-2, like its predecessor, is principles-based supervisory guidance that examiners apply through judgment rather than as an enforceable rule. By this post's reading, SR 26-2's effective challenge standard offers the closest available analogy for how supervisors are likely to evaluate agentic system governance, even though SR 26-2 explicitly excludes agentic AI from scope. The [effective-challenge post](post.html?slug=effective-challenge) covers what that standard imposes on validators. Most banking regulatory vocabulary maps onto this regime: "effective challenge," "qualified personnel," "expert judgment," "independent validation." Regulators have clear expectations here, even if those expectations were written for static, deterministic models.

**Runtime HITL** is the approval gate, the interrupt checkpoint, the confidence-threshold escalation, the kill switch. This is what FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report is asking about when it mentions "where to have 'human in the loop' agent oversight protocols."[^1] It involves a different human, a different control surface, and a different failure mode from deploy-time oversight. It is also the regime that existing regulatory frameworks address least specifically. SR 26-2's GenAI exclusion means there is no governing directive on what runtime HITL should look like for agentic systems.

The regulatory vocabulary mismatch runs deepest here. Regulators primarily specify deploy-time expectations. ML engineers primarily build runtime mechanisms. Those are not substitutes, and the asymmetry between them is sharper than the binary HITL question suggests. A well-validated agent with no runtime checkpoint operates within bounds someone with standing has thought through; an agent with a runtime checkpoint but no deploy-time validation operates within bounds nobody has tested. The first case is missing a control surface. The second is missing the question of whether any control surface would work, which is the more dangerous gap, because nothing in the runtime stack is set up to surface it.

## The Counterargument: Just Do Runtime

The coherent frontline response to deploy-time MRM apparatus is to abandon most of it. Modern observability, runtime guardrails, structured evals, and LLM-as-judge monitoring stacks can in many cases substitute for nine-month conceptual-soundness reviews and the inventory-and-validation machinery that's grown up around SR 11-7. For a foundation-model deployment whose underlying weights update on a vendor's schedule the bank doesn't control, much of what deploy-time validation tries to certify is obsolete the moment it's documented. A bank running continuous adversarial testing, hard deterministic guardrails at output, and inference-time drift monitoring can plausibly claim a stronger evidentiary record than one whose model documentation packet was produced eighteen months ago and has not been stress-tested since.

This argument is not silly. It's the position the [Bank Policy Institute's April 2025 repeal letter](https://bpi.com/the-most-damaging-guidance-in-banking/) made explicit, and one the SR 26-2 supersession partly accommodated by excluding GenAI and agentic systems from scope. It also reflects a real failure of the legacy MRM apparatus: nine-month review cycles, model inventories in five-digit counts, validation packs that get rubber-stamped because the validators lack the technical depth to challenge what they're reviewing. The [effective-challenge post](post.html?slug=effective-challenge) argues that this apparatus is procedure mistaken for principle. The runtime-only argument is the strongest available response if you accept that diagnosis and conclude the principle was never the load-bearing piece.

Where it breaks is on a question runtime monitoring cannot answer: whether the runtime mechanisms themselves were designed to function under the conditions the system will actually operate in. That requires someone with the technical knowledge to evaluate the runtime architecture, the incentives to ask the uncomfortable feasibility question, and the organizational authority to delay rollout if the answer is no. Runtime monitoring assumes the monitoring works. Knight Capital is the case where that assumption was the failure.

It also breaks on action classes where individual decisions hit consumers and become irreversible before any monitoring flag fires. Wire transfers and RTP/FedNow instant payments settle in seconds with no chargeback mechanism; an AI payment agent that misroutes a transaction under a runtime-only oversight model has no recovery path, and the consumer protection gaps are worst on exactly these rails.[^2] The EU AI Act's Article 14 obligations push toward calibrated oversight that names a deploy-time owner. The article applies from 2 August 2026 to high-risk systems (including creditworthiness assessment of natural persons), and binds banks with EU market exposure. It also serves as a directional indicator for where US rulemaking is likely to follow. MAS's November 2025 consultation paper, still in draft form, ties required human oversight to how autonomous the system is and how heavily users rely on it; task complexity is a third input. None of those have a runtime answer.

The emerging supervisory template requires both regimes calibrated together. Calibration depends on the autonomy of the system and the irreversibility of its outputs; consumer-facing actions raise the bar further. One party designs the runtime checkpoint; another operates it. The runtime-only argument, like the deploy-time-only argument before it, was asking the wrong question.

## A Worked Case: Knight Capital's HITL Failure

Knight Capital appears in [the A2A case studies post](post.html?slug=a2a-case-studies) for what it reveals about kill switches and dead code as attack surfaces; that's a different lesson from the same event. The HITL failure deserves separate treatment here.

Knight's human oversight architecture on August 1, 2012 was not absent. It consisted of two elements: 97 automated "Power Peg disabled" error emails sent to personnel in the 89 minutes before market open, and PMON, a real-time position monitoring tool staffed by human operators during trading hours. Both constituted human oversight in the compliance documentation. Neither functioned as effective runtime oversight in practice.

The emails weren't designed as system alerts. Personnel received them in the 89 minutes before markets opened, when intervention was still possible, and did not act on them. Once trading began, PMON relied entirely on manual surveillance of raw data, generated no automated alerts, and became functionally useless at the transaction volume of August 1. In 45 minutes the system executed 4 million trades across 154 stocks while human operators watched a monitoring tool that could not process what was happening fast enough to surface a flag. The SEC's finding: Knight's controls were "not reasonably designed to manage the risk of its market access."[^3]

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The SEC found Knight's risk management system "not reasonably designed" despite being formally present. The standard is not whether a human oversight mechanism exists. The standard is whether the mechanism is designed to function under the conditions the system will actually operate in. Answering that requires deploy-time design work; runtime monitoring will not produce the answer once the system is live. Any HITL design for an agentic system has to settle the question before the first production transaction.

Read this way, Knight had runtime HITL in form. PMON and the alert pipeline were both nominal human-in-the-loop mechanisms. What was missing was the deploy-time governance work that would have stress-tested whether either would function under the load the system was always going to encounter.

An effective challenge function backed by [its three pillars](post.html?slug=effective-challenge) would have asked three questions before SMARS went live. None are runtime questions; all are deploy-time questions about whether the runtime design would hold.

A *competence* question would have asked whether anyone with the technical depth to evaluate the alert pipeline had confirmed that "Power Peg disabled" emails would surface as a stop signal at production volume, or whether validation had accepted "alerts are configured" as sufficient evidence. At Knight, the design assumption that 97 messages in 89 minutes would prompt intervention was never tested by anyone with standing to test it. Validators with the systems-engineering background to model the alert pipeline against the cognitive load of the operators receiving it appear to have been absent from the deployment review.

The *influence* test was whether a written escalation procedure with a named owner could halt trading, and whether that procedure had been rehearsed under conditions analogous to August 1. PMON operators had monitoring authority. Who could stop the trading system, and on what evidence, was not crisply answered before the event. Without influence, monitoring becomes a passive activity rather than an intervention surface; that is what makes runtime authority real rather than nominal.

The *incentives* probe is whether the validation function was rewarded for raising the uncomfortable feasibility question (*what is PMON's throughput ceiling, and what happens at the open of an unusually heavy trading day?*), or whether that question was structurally suppressed by the deployment schedule. Speed-to-market pressure on a first-line technology team without an independent challenge function with standing to delay is the institutional condition under which uncomfortable questions don't get asked.

All three are deploy-time questions. None of them can be answered by runtime monitoring after the fact. A runtime-only governance posture leaves them structurally orphaned: no function in the institution owns them, and the failure mode is precisely Knight's. Runtime mechanisms exist in form and collapse in function under the load the system was always going to encounter. For banks deploying AI agents in credit, payments, or trading, a HITL framework that does not designate a deploy-time owner for the runtime checkpoint design has not solved Knight Capital's problem. It has reproduced the documentation of it.

## The Checkpoint Problem in Multi-Agent Systems

Runtime HITL in a multi-agent pipeline has a structural problem that the Knight Capital framing clarifies but doesn't fully capture. A human sitting at a checkpoint between agents 2 and 3 in a five-agent pipeline evaluates an intermediate artifact produced by reasoning they cannot inspect, and makes a decision whose consequences depend on what agents 3 through 5 will do next. Even with a well-designed alert and explicit authority, the information available at the checkpoint is partial by design.

The ACPR identified the behavioral mechanism in 2020: humans feel greater responsibility when *contradicting* an algorithm than when *confirming* it.[^4] At a checkpoint displaying a model's confident recommendation, the path of least cognitive resistance is approval. This is not a failure of any individual reviewer; it's a predictable consequence of how the checkpoint is constructed. Designing a runtime HITL gate without accounting for automation bias is designing a rubber stamp with better documentation.

The Air France 447 crash (BEA final report, 2012) named the same dynamic in a different domain.[^5] The autopilot disconnected correctly; the HITL design worked as specified. The crew was incapable of using the manual control they'd been handed because years of reliable automation had eroded their diagnostic competence. The BEA's conclusion: "most probably this crew and most crews were not prepared to face such an event." Model validation teams that have never been required to challenge a well-performing model, never to reproduce its reasoning, never to override a conclusion, are accumulating the same deficit. When a novel distribution shift or adversarial input finally arrives, it comes in a system the validator has never practiced disagreeing with.

A 2012 meta-analysis in JAMIA across 74 studies found automation bias robustly documented across experience levels and task types, with override rates rarely exceeding 10–15% even in research settings where participants knew they were being studied.[^6] A human checkpoint with a consistent 5% override rate is not providing independent oversight. It is providing the appearance of it, and the legal exposure is arguably worse than no checkpoint at all.

Better checkpoint design is necessary but may not be sufficient on its own. The organizational complement sits outside checkpoint architecture but inside governance design: incentive structures that don't penalize override, second-review requirements for high-confidence approvals, periodic exercises where validators practice challenging well-performing models. A checkpoint that fires correctly but whose reviewers have career incentives to approve has not solved the Knight Capital problem.

The liability posture compounds the engineering one. A documented HITL checkpoint where a human approved a bad output is evidentiary in a fair lending dispute or a Regulation E error-resolution proceeding; "human reviewed and confirmed" becomes evidence of the bank's role in the bad outcome rather than a defense against it. The checkpoint has to surface what the human needs to exercise genuine judgment. A checkpoint that only presents the model's conclusion for endorsement produces a rubber stamp. Whether any given institution's HITL governance addresses these distinctions is a harder question than whether the checkpoint exists.

## Calibration: The Proportionality Framework

The most concrete framework available is MAS's November 2025 consultation paper, still in draft form: quantum of human oversight = f(autonomy, complexity, reliance), where reliance is defined as "the level of autonomy granted to the AI system or model, the degree of human involvement or oversight in the process it supports, as well as the availability of alternatives." This makes oversight a calibrated design variable rather than a binary presence-or-absence question, though the consultation paper names the variables without resolving the function. The calibration work remains, institution by institution, for each action class.

OSFI Guideline E-23 (Canadian prudential regulator, effective May 2027; cited here as a comparative reference only) goes further by making autonomy a model-risk *rating factor*, feeding the oversight question directly into the model's overall risk tier rather than treating it as a separate operational control.[^7] BaFin's 2021 principles articulate the most operational expression: human involvement intensifies as algorithmic output diverges from baseline, with the guidance implying that decisions should remain reversible within defined windows. The reversibility window is the element most directly relevant to agentic payment systems. Knowing when an action remains reversible determines where the runtime gate has to sit; existence of a gate is a separate question.

An updated version of the guardrails tier table's HITL column would specify for each tier which timing regime applies, what the human's defined authority is at each point, and whether a reversibility window exists. A specified row reads as "Deploy-time: quarterly validation + ongoing monitoring; Runtime: mandate-scope interrupt before wire or RTP execution; Reversibility: ACH alternative required before irrevocable rail commitment." The checkbox summarizes the design rather than substituting for it.

What I'm watching is whether any institution can get the checkpoint construction right at the level Knight Capital's failure made necessary. The interrupt firing is the easy part. The harder question is what the human sees when it fires, and whether the audit trail of that decision would survive both a supervisory examination and a consumer dispute. Practiced judgment matters more than the existence of the checkpoint. The oversight exists on paper at most institutions. Whether it functions under the conditions that actually matter is a different question, and it is the one that examiners, and eventually courts, are beginning to ask.
