> **TL;DR:** Effective challenge — SR 11-7's requirement that models face critical analysis from independent parties who are structurally separated from development, technically capable of probing model behavior, and organizationally empowered to force change — is the one insight from 2011 that survives the transition to generative and agentic AI. Each leg of that triad is under acute stress: first-line pay dominance corrupts incentives, model opacity undermines competence, and vendor-controlled release cycles weaken influence. The question isn't whether to repeal SR 11-7. It's whether effective challenge can be preserved without the procedural apparatus that's come to stand in for it.

---

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Regulatory context (April 2026):** SR 11-7 was formally superseded by [joint Fed/OCC/FDIC guidance SR 26-2](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/SR2602.htm) on April 17, 2026, resolving the BPI repeal debate in an unexpected way: not repeal, but supersession, with these systems **explicitly excluded from scope** (footnote 3). What that exclusion means in practice is unsettled — it does not clearly exempt GenAI deployments from all model risk requirements. The guidance is principles-based and primarily scoped to banks above $30B in assets; smaller institutions face less direct regulatory pressure from the supersession but inherit the same conceptual vacuum on GenAI governance. The effective challenge triad this post argues for remains the most relevant organizing concept for GenAI governance precisely because the new guidance leaves the question formally unaddressed. Expect the framework to keep shifting.

In April 2025, the Bank Policy Institute called for the [outright repeal of SR 11-7](https://bpi.com/the-most-damaging-guidance-in-banking/), the 2011 supervisory letter that governs model risk management at every major US bank. It's a reasonable position to argue. The framework was built for linear regressions and closed-form analytics and has accreted, by BPI's own estimate, "thousands, perhaps tens of thousands" of compliance jobs.

What made the debate strange was the response. BPI's own member banks (or at least their senior model risk executives) [publicly told *Risk.net*](https://www.risk.net/risk-management/7963135/bpi-says-sr-11-7-should-go-bank-model-risk-chiefs-say-%E2%80%98no%E2%80%99) that repeal would weaken risk management. A trade association arguing for deregulation while the people doing the regulated work argued against is a real-money disagreement about what's load-bearing and what isn't.

I think that disagreement is about one specific phrase: **effective challenge**. It's SR 11-7's founding organizing principle. It's also, in my read, the only piece of the 2011 framework whose underlying insight survives the transition to foundation models and autonomous agents. That judgment became more urgent when SR 11-7 was formally superseded by SR 26-2 in April 2026: the new joint guidance explicitly excludes these systems from scope, leaving effective challenge for GenAI formally unaddressed and making the triad more load-bearing as an organizing concept than it was when SR 11-7 was the governing framework. Annual validation cycles, [reported nine-month delays](https://bpi.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/BPI-OSTP-AI-RFI-Response-10.27.25.pdf) for AI use cases, and model inventories measured in five-digit counts are procedure that grew up around the principle and are now being mistaken for it.[^1]

Which requires being precise about what effective challenge actually is, and what it has to become when the model is non-deterministic, the internals are opaque, and the vendor updates it on a cadence nobody at the bank controls.

## What effective challenge actually means

SR 11-7 defines effective challenge as "critical analysis by objective, informed parties who can identify model limitations and assumptions and produce appropriate changes." The definition sounds procedural, but the guidance is explicit that it rests on three qualities: **incentives** (separation from development, supported by compensation and culture), **competence** (technical knowledge to evaluate), and **influence** (organizational authority over model deployment, backed by senior management commitment).

That triad is the concept's actual substance. Independent review (the term SR 11-7 replaced) only required structural separation. Effective challenge adds the claim that structural separation without the other two legs produces compliance theater. Validators can report to the CRO, publish findings, and still fail to force any change in a model's use if they lack the authority or standing to do so.

The OCC has since unified the terminology. The [2021 Comptroller's Handbook on Model Risk Management](https://web.archive.org/web/20250222103442/https://www.occ.gov/publications-and-resources/publications/comptrollers-handbook/files/model-risk-management/pub-ch-model-risk.pdf)[^2] states explicitly that "effective challenge is synonymous with credible challenge" — the phrase the OCC's 2014 [Heightened Standards](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/federal-register/2014/nr-occ-2014-117a.pdf) introduced to flag that some challenges are performative and others substantive. The shift was subtle but consequential. Supervisors had watched banks satisfy structural requirements for a decade and still miss material model problems. That shift from asking "does an independent team exist?" to "is the challenge actually producing risk reduction?" is the most significant evolution of the concept since 2011, and the one most at risk in the repeal debate. Dismantling the procedural scaffolding is cheap. Recovering the substantive expectation once it's gone is expensive.

## Why 2011 was a genuine conceptual upgrade

The predecessor guidance, [OCC Bulletin 2000-16](https://www.occ.gov/static/rescinded-bulletins/bulletin-2000-16.pdf), required that validation personnel be "as independent as possible" from model developers. That phrase was aspirational; it offered no framework for what made independence effective. Banks could satisfy the letter of the requirement while validation remained structurally ineffective — I've [written about how that played out in the previous post](post.html?slug=sr11-7).

SR 11-7's contribution was to identify the specific ways independence fails. The guidance's warning on [asymmetric user challenges](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107a1.pdf) — that model users "tend to be less likely to challenge an outcome that results in an advantage for them" — is the 2011 document in miniature: a crisp, organizationally sophisticated claim that human incentives corrupt validation unless the structure deliberately counteracts them.

The triad is the operational answer to that problem. It's also what every other major bank regulator has converged on, with or without the phrase. The [UK PRA's SS1/23](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss) (May 2023) adopted "effective challenge" verbatim and treats model risk as "a risk in its own right." The [ECB's Guide to Internal Models](https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/ecb/pub/pdf/ssm.guidetointernalmodels_consolidated_202310~16db14dfb7.en.pdf) uses "effective independent challenge." [Canada's OSFI Guideline E-23](https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/guideline-e-23-model-risk-management-2027) (final September 2025, effective May 2027) uses "independent assessment" but keeps the three-leg substance intact.[^3] Australia's APRA is the conspicuous outlier, with no dedicated MRM prudential standard at all. The Basel Committee has never issued a standalone MRM principles paper.

The terminological divergence matters less than the convergence on the underlying claim. When five jurisdictions arrive at the same triad from different regulatory traditions, the concept is probably real.

## Each leg of the triad is under acute stress

The reason effective challenge survives conceptually isn't that it's untouched by GenAI. It's that the three failure modes it was designed to prevent — perverse incentives, inadequate competence, missing influence — are exactly the failure modes that foundation models and autonomous agents amplify. The framework diagnoses the right problem. The apparatus built to address it was calibrated for a different technology.

### Incentives

The [BIS Financial Stability Institute's 2024 paper on model risk](https://www.bis.org/fsi/publ/insights66.pdf) identified an asymmetric competence-and-compensation gap: "Remuneration and experience in first line functions are still considerably higher and more senior than in second line functions." When first-line model builders are better paid and more senior than the validators challenging them, "effective challenge" is a polite fiction regardless of what the org chart says.

GenAI makes the asymmetry worse in two specific ways. First, the practitioners who genuinely understand foundation models are expensive and scarce, and hiring competition is primarily with first-line teams.

Second, vendor dependence introduces a new asymmetry. BPI documents cases where "some banks have opted to ask vendors to remove or turn off AI features in third-party products in order to obviate the need for a lengthy and unpredictable MRM review." That's effective challenge working in the worst possible way: the process is so burdensome that the business-side incentive is to make models disappear rather than govern them.[^4]

Third, and least discussed, the boundary of what counts as first-line is shifting. In many institutions, GenAI deployment is increasingly routed through Technology organizations that historically operated under software development lifecycle governance rather than model risk management. SDLC prioritizes iterative release, which is a reasonable default for most software but corrosive for models.

When a foundation model arrives as a vendor API feature rather than as a discrete model development project, the institutional question of who owns its model risk is not reliably resolved by any current framework. The effective challenge triad was designed to challenge a quantitative modeling function that had internalized, however imperfectly, the expectation of being challenged. Technology functions have not absorbed that expectation, and the gatekeeping position they've inherited through vendor-mediated GenAI adoption is a structural shift the current MRM framework has not yet acknowledged.

### Competence

SR 11-7 requires validators to understand "how and why" a model produces its outputs. The [Swiss regulator FINMA has stated directly](https://www.finma.ch/en/~/media/finma/dokumente/dokumentencenter/myfinma/4dokumentation/finma-publikationen/aufsichtsmitteilungen/20231218-finma-aufsichtsmitteilung-08-2023.pdf?sc_lang=en) that "some AI model results cannot be understood, explained or reproduced and therefore cannot be critically assessed." For foundation models accessed through an API, this isn't a soft limitation — it's a structural one.

Non-determinism compounds the problem. [Deloitte UK's analysis](https://www2.deloitte.com/content/dam/Deloitte/uk/Documents/financial-services/deloitte-uk-gen-ai-in-financial-services.pdf) points out that LLMs produce different outputs for identical inputs; the variation can be material. Traditional validation testing assumes input-output reproducibility; competence-leg challenge assumes the validator can trace effects to causes.

Neither holds for an LLM where a prompt phrasing change can alter behavior and where the model may itself [alter its responses when it detects it's being tested](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.09390). The 2011 definition of competence was a skill problem. In 2026 it's partly an epistemic one.

### Influence

The hardest leg. Influence is what SR 11-7 meant by the board-level structures, senior management accountability, and authority to force change. It assumed a model with a clear owner, a clear lifecycle, and the possibility of retirement or remediation.

Foundation-model governance breaks each assumption. [The vendor controls the release cadence](post.html?slug=sr11-7) — a bank cannot "retire" an LLM when the underlying weights are updated on the vendor's schedule. Agentic systems that chain multiple models introduce [emergent behaviors that cross ownership lines](post.html?slug=a2a-risks). A validator with the authority to block deployment of a single static scorecard does not obviously have the authority to block an agentic pipeline whose components are individually approved.

The influence leg is also the one most exposed to top-down pressure. Effective challenge depends on senior management accepting a validator's authority and actively defending it when a business owner pushes back. C-suite mandates for rapid agentic deployment — now a widespread feature of large-bank AI strategy — operate, whether or not by design, as a signal that second-line authority is subordinate to adoption velocity. Structural independence does not substitute for senior management willingness to actually invoke it, and a third line evaluating that dynamic is only as credible as the mandate backing it.

The ownership question the [guardrails post](post.html?slug=guardrails) left deliberately open is the influence leg's institutional manifestation: second-line authority available on paper and absent in practice.

## The London Whale was never a competence failure

The 2012 JPMorgan trading loss — $6.2 billion, caused by a new VaR model that cut reported risk by roughly 50% while actual risk was escalating — remains the clearest operational demonstration of what effective challenge is protecting against. The Chief Investment Office's new model was built by [someone who had never built a VaR model before](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2013/01/24/the-jp-morgan-whale-report-and-the-ghosts-of-the-financial-crisis/). A subsequent Excel error used SUM instead of AVERAGE in a volatility calculation, and over 300 VaR limit breaches were resolved by swapping in a more lenient model rather than investigating the underlying trades.[^5]

Risk managers involved later reported feeling "not sufficiently independent to challenge the office's practices." The structural separation was there. The incentives leg was not. The post-mortem doesn't read like a technical failure — it reads like an influence failure with a technical veneer, which is exactly the failure mode SR 11-7 was written to prevent and which it clearly did not prevent.

## The case for repeal, steelmanned

The argument isn't that the BPI critique is wrong on its premises. Tens of thousands of people do work jobs whose primary output is confirming compliance with the Model Risk guidance, and examiner interpretation is wildly inconsistent.

An [EY/GARP white paper](https://www.garp.org/hubfs/Whitepapers/a2r8d000003N6bbAAC_WP-Subrahmanyam.pdf) by Subrahmanyam OV and Surabhi Kashyap argues, with some support, that "MRM, which was created with the sole objective to provide an independent assessment of the models, has inadvertently been transformed into a process-driven activity," and that COVID-era model failures went through MRM functions that "approved all those models."

The [2019 GAO ruling](https://www.gao.gov/products/b-330843) that SR 11-7 constitutes a "rule" under the Congressional Review Act but was never submitted to Congress is a real legal exposure — the GAO finding is advisory, but it opens a compliance question no regulator has since resolved. The [OCC's Bulletin 2025-26](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html) itself acknowledged that "even small, less complex banks have conducted full annual model validation or established in-house modeling teams... without regard to whether it is reasonable in light of the banks' risk profiles." Over-application to low-risk models is a real phenomenon.

None of that makes the triad the problem. The apparatus built around the triad is the problem. Repealing SR 11-7 to fix the apparatus is the wrong operation on the wrong tissue — it removes the framework's conceptual spine to deal with scar tissue from how the spine has been applied. Most regulatory overhauls that start that way end with the original insight still needed and no framework for enforcing it.

## The adaptations that might actually preserve the triad

The policy conversation is converging — though not yet committing — on a set of adaptations that retain the triad while rebuilding the apparatus. Most of them appear in some form in the [Treasury's February 2026 Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0401) and in [Acting Comptroller Hsu's FSOC 2024 speech](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/speeches/2024/pub-speech-2024-67.pdf) on AI's evolutionary path from input to co-pilot to autonomous agent.

**AI-augmented oversight**, where LLMs monitor other LLMs against compliance assertions, is intellectually the most interesting and operationally the most fragile. The monitoring LLM is itself a model under SR 11-7, which creates a regress the framework wasn't designed to handle. **Red-teaming as a formal challenge mechanism** — adversarial testing of LLMs and agents by a structurally independent team — maps onto the competence leg for a failure mode (prompt injection, jailbreaking, goal hijacking) that traditional validation has no way to probe, and that the [guardrails post](post.html?slug=guardrails) argued has no current regulatory anchor.

**Risk-tiered governance** is the adaptation that most closely tracks the 2021 Comptroller's Handbook's shift to substantive expectations. Control intensity calibrated to materiality and use case would address the community-bank over-application the OCC itself flagged, while preserving full-triad challenge for capital-markets and credit applications where it genuinely matters. Wells Fargo researchers have [proposed mandatory kill switches and pre-established disconnection metrics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15668) — a governance artifact SR 11-7 did not anticipate because static models don't generally need them.[^6]

**Champion-challenger frameworks as standard practice** — running alternative models in parallel with production models — were an exceptional measure in 2011 and are plausibly the default for any non-trivial AI deployment by 2028. The shift from **periodic validation to continuous monitoring** addresses the non-determinism and drift problem at the apparatus level rather than the concept level. Challenge becomes ongoing rather than annual.

None of these are settled. Michael Barr's [April 2025 San Francisco Fed speech](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/barr20250404a.htm) cited SR 11-7 as the applicable framework for GenAI while simultaneously signaling its insufficiency. The FDIC's [2026 House testimony](https://docs.house.gov/meetings/BA/BA21/20260326/119110/HHRG-119-BA21-Wstate-BillingsleyR-20260326.pdf) promised "a more tailored, risk-based approach." SR 26-2's April 2026 issuance is the formal response to both: a joint Fed/OCC/FDIC revision that adopts risk-based tiering while explicitly carving these systems out of scope. The [May 2025 GAO finding](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107197) that no US regulator had issued AI-specific MRM guidance remains effectively true for GenAI. Active review with no formal resolution is, as of this writing, still the state of GenAI model risk governance.

## What I think holds, and what I don't

What I think I know: the triad is load-bearing. The failures that motivated SR 11-7 in 2011 (asymmetric incentives, inadequate competence, absent influence) are exactly the failures that GenAI sharpens. Dropping the organizing concept to escape the compliance bureaucracy that has grown around it solves the second problem by creating a larger first one. Credible challenge is not a ceremony. It is what prevents a $6.2 billion loss from a VaR model built by someone who had never built one. That's not evidence the triad prevents every failure (the COVID-era MRM approvals are a counterweight), but it's evidence that removing the triad removes the only structural mechanism designed to catch it.

What I don't know: whether any of the proposed adaptations can deliver substantive challenge in practice. Continuous monitoring can become a dashboard that generates signals no second-line team has the standing to escalate. Red-teaming can default to the same first-line team running probes against a catalog they know the guardrails already block.

Champion-challenger can become two models maintained by the same team. AI-augmented oversight can become an opaque system reviewing another opaque system, with nobody able to audit either. Each adaptation reproduces the same calibration problem (the apparatus standing in for the principle) in a new medium.

The piece I'm watching most closely is the **influence leg**. It was the hardest leg in 2011 and it's harder now. In practice, "authority to force change" on a foundation model means the authority to tell a business owner they cannot deploy a vendor product that the vendor is actively shipping to their competitors. Nothing in the current regulatory conversation, US or international, tells me where that authority will live or how it will be exercised. Without a concrete answer there, the repeal debate is debating a framework that isn't yet adequate to the present, never mind the next five years.

The triad emerged from 2008 because the failures of 2008 made it unavoidable. SR 26-2's exclusion of GenAI from scope is, in that framing, a deferral: the regulatory apparatus stepped back from the hardest question rather than working through it. The debate has shifted from whether to repeal the framework to what fills the gap its supersession left open for GenAI. Whether the next forcing function is a high-profile agentic failure, a foreign regulatory development that US banks have to match, or a quiet examiner enforcement action that creates case law, something will eventually supply the pressure that 2026's formal guidance did not. The practical question is whether effective challenge will have been preserved as an organizing concept by then, or treated as a relic of the pre-2026 apparatus and discarded along with it. The [adversarial testing series](post.html?slug=adversarial-workflow) is where that question gets operational: what effective challenge actually requires practitioners to build and staff for a GenAI deployment, and whether the structural conditions for it currently exist.
