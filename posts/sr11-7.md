> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Update — April 17, 2026:** SR 11-7 has been formally superseded. The Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC jointly issued [Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management (SR 26-2)](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr2602.htm) — the first comprehensive, tri-agency revision to the foundational MRM framework since 2011. The new guidance adopts a more risk-based, tiered approach and is primarily scoped to banking organizations with more than $30 billion in total assets. **Generative AI and agentic AI are explicitly excluded from scope** — the agencies describe these technologies as "novel and rapidly evolving" and state they are "not within the scope of this guidance," while noting that a banking organization's existing risk management and governance practices should guide appropriate controls for such systems. The broader questions this post raises about MRM frameworks for GenAI and agentic systems remain formally unaddressed by regulators.

I started my career in credit risk management in June 2009, joining a large bank's second-line risk rotational program. I nearly didn't make it to my first day. About two weeks before orientation, PBS Frontline aired a [documentary](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/frontline/documentary/breakingthebank/) asking whether my soon-to-be employer was about to break — my concern wasn't losing the offer, it was whether there'd be a bank left to report to.[^1]

That documentary planted a question I'd spend the next several years answering from the inside: how had the models everyone trusted gotten so catastrophically wrong? And why had almost no one been in a position, organizationally or culturally, to stop it?

> [!TIP]
> **The three lines of defense** — a risk governance structure that banks adopted widely after the crisis and that SR 11-7 helped codify — divides accountability into three layers. The first line is the business: the line of business that builds and uses models, earns revenue from them, and formally owns the associated risk. The second line is independent risk oversight: functions like model risk management that are structurally separated from the business, with incentives oriented toward identifying and escalating problems rather than approving them. The third line is internal audit, whose job is to verify that the first and second lines are actually doing what they're supposed to. The incentive asymmetries between these three layers — a first line rewarded for growth, a second line rewarded for prudence, a third line rewarded for catching gaps — are central to how SR 11-7 thinks about "effective challenge."

My first rotation was credit card credit risk — where I arrived in June 2009 to watch charge-off rates peak almost exactly on schedule, roughly six months after Lehman fell. I then moved into counterparty credit modeling, where MRM was gaining real institutional traction but the systems were drowning in data errors from merger integration.

After finishing my program in the summer of 2011, I stayed in second-line, working on small business credit risk management — exactly when SR 11-7 was being finalized. I watched MRM transform in real time: from a back-office validation function into an independent governing body with real teeth, staffed to structurally reward for identifying problems.[^2]

By 2013 I'd had enough of being the second line "fun police" and moved into auto lending to build models — only to hit a different wall. The MRM overhead was heavy, and the culture was locked into proprietary tooling (e.g., SAS) at exactly the moment the field was discovering Python and R. I left for a master's in data science and eventually a PhD. When I came back to banking in 2019 — this time building NLP systems in Technology — I found out what that friction felt like in the emerging world of AI.

Which is why I find the current moment so interesting — and a little unnerving. [SR 11-7](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm), issued jointly by the Fed and OCC on April 4, 2011, remains the foundational U.S. framework for model risk management in banking — and it has never been formally updated. The framework was designed for an era of linear regression models and closed-form analytics.

Today, as large language models generate text rather than point estimates and agentic AI makes autonomous multi-step actions, SR 11-7's core assumptions are straining. Regulators have begun signaling change — the [OCC announced a broader MRM review in September 2025](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html); [Governor Barr explicitly called for updating standards in April 2025](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/barr20250404a.htm) — but no formal revision has materialized. The industry is applying 2011 principles to 2026 technology, waiting for regulatory clarity that may not arrive before the next wave of AI adoption reshapes the field.

## The crisis-era failures that made SR 11-7 inevitable

The story of SR 11-7 begins not in 2011 but in the cascading model failures of the preceding decade. Long-Term Capital Management's 1998 collapse — driven by [Value-at-Risk (VaR)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_at_risk) models that assumed constant volatility and stable correlations — offered an early warning the industry largely failed to heed.[^3] Models calibrated on benign historical periods could catastrophically underestimate tail risk. The lesson was clear but went unlearned.

The 2007–2009 financial crisis delivered that lesson at systemic scale. [David Li's Gaussian copula model](https://www.wired.com/2009/02/wp-quant/), published in 2000, encoded an empirically fatal assumption: that default correlations were constant and simultaneous extreme defaults were nearly impossible. When the housing market turned, CDO tranche correlations that had been modeled at 0.2–0.3 surged to nearly 1.0. Moody's 2005 models predicted worst-case subprime default rates of 5%; by 2008, actual rates exceeded 20%.[^4] AIG Financial Products sold credit default swap protection at near-zero premiums — a position that ultimately required a $182 billion government bailout.

VaR models failed not because the mathematics was wrong but because the assumptions were inappropriate. [Nassim Taleb, testifying before Congress in 2009](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-111hhrg51925/pdf/CHRG-111hhrg51925.pdf): "VaR does not replicate out of sample — the past almost never predicts subsequent blowups." [Emanuel Derman had warned as early as 1996](http://pricing.online.fr/docs/model_risk.pdf): "There are always implicit assumptions behind a model and its solution method. But human beings have limited foresight and great imagination, so that, inevitably, a model will be used in ways its creator never intended." Before SR 11-7, the regulatory framework for addressing any of this was thin — [OCC 2000-16](https://www.occ.gov/static/rescinded-bulletins/bulletin-2000-16.pdf) (2000) introduced independence validation but that validation simply "should be as independent as possible." Development practices, governance structures, and enterprise-wide model risk appetite were left unaddressed.

## How three pillars and one definition reshaped an industry

SR 11-7 rests on three pillars that became the organizing framework for every major bank's MRM program. The first — sound model development, implementation, and use — requires a clear statement of purpose, rigorous methodology, and documentation detailed enough for an unfamiliar party to understand the model's operation and limitations. The second — rigorous model validation — specifies three core elements: evaluation of conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, and outcomes analysis (backtesting, sensitivity analysis, benchmarking against alternatives). The third — governance, policies, and controls — requires board-level oversight, senior management accountability, and a full model inventory.

Two definitional choices had outsized consequences. First, SR 11-7 defines a model as "a quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process input data into **quantitative estimates**." That phrase — *quantitative estimates* — becomes a central point of contention when the output is a paragraph rather than a number. Second, the guidance established "effective challenge": critical analysis by objective, informed parties who can identify limitations and effect change. This was transformational because it went beyond procedural compliance to require that validators possess genuine authority to push back — and that organizations empower them to do so.

The industry response was massive. Large banks created dedicated MRM functions, organized around a three-lines-of-defense structure. Model inventories grew to between 1,000 and 10,000+ models at large institutions, expanding at 10–25% annually.[^5] Board-level Model Risk Committees became standard. The framework that had been a back-office function became, in the span of a few years, a significant governing institution.

## A decade of implementation exposed both the framework's power and its limits

The most dramatic early test came with [JPMorgan Chase's "London Whale" incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_JPMorgan_Chase_trading_loss) in 2012 — over $6 billion in trading losses from a portfolio of synthetic credit derivatives. The root cause was a textbook model risk governance failure: a VaR model for the Chief Investment Office was modified in a way that dramatically understated risk, and internal controls failed to detect it. The OCC assessed a [$300 million civil money penalty](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2013/nr-occ-2013-140.html) in September 2013. The episode became the defining case study for why SR 11-7's emphasis on independent challenge and ongoing monitoring mattered.

The [CECL standard (FASB ASU 2016-13)](https://www.fasb.org/page/document?pdf=ASU+2016-13.pdf&title=UPDATE%202016-13%E2%80%94FINANCIAL%20INSTRUMENTS%E2%80%94CREDIT%20LOSSES%20(TOPIC%20326):%20MEASUREMENT%20OF%20CREDIT%20LOSSES%20ON%20FINANCIAL%20INSTRUMENTS) created an unusually demanding test. Moving from incurred-loss to forward-looking, lifetime expected loss estimates dramatically increased model complexity. When COVID-19 struck in early 2020, CECL models that had barely been implemented were immediately overwhelmed: [82% of banks reported credit-risk models were most heavily affected, and 66% resorted to management overlays](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/risk-and-resilience/our-insights/a-strategic-vision-for-model-risk-management) that initially lacked adequate governance controls of their own.

Vendor and third-party models became a persistent challenge. SR 11-7 maintained that vendor models must be incorporated into the same MRM framework as in-house models — but banks validating proprietary products can't replicate or fully inspect the underlying methodology. That compromise becomes far more consequential in the AI era, where the vendor is OpenAI or Anthropic and the product is a foundation model accessed through an API.

## Why generative and agentic AI break the SR 11-7 framework

SR 11-7 was designed for models with defined parameters, reproducible outputs, and behavior that could be reconstructed after the fact. Modern AI systems are dynamic rather than static,[^11] probabilistic rather than deterministic, and in some cases agentic (capable of pursuing objectives with limited human intervention). This is not a marginal extension but a categorical shift that challenges each of the three pillars.

The interpretability problem strikes directly at conceptual soundness. For a neural network with billions of parameters, or an LLM trained on petabytes of text, "conceptual soundness" has no clear analog. LLMs introduce stochasticity and prompt sensitivity with no precedent in traditional MRM. The same prompt submitted twice can produce different outputs; small wording variations can dramatically alter model behavior.

I ran into an early version of this problem between 2019 and 2021, doing NLP work in a banking context. What I experienced was a round peg being forced into a very square hole: an MRM process designed for statistical models — defined parameters, backtestable outputs, a clear performance metric — being applied to text classifiers where none of those things mapped cleanly. The most vivid illustration was an 18-month review process for a text classifier to flag compliance issues for human review. Eighteen months. For a model that by any reasonable standard of complexity (i.e., human in full control) sat well below the threshold that should have triggered full-scale validation. By the time the review started, the model sponsor lost interest and the business had already moved on.

The definitional question remains unresolved. SR 11-7 covers systems that produce "quantitative estimates" — but an LLM generating a credit memo summary or a regulatory filing draft produces text rather than point estimates. The Consumer Bankers Association flagged this directly in 2025: it's unclear whether text generated as a result of probabilistic processes constitutes a "quantitative estimate" under the current definition.[^6] If it doesn't, large swaths of AI deployment could exist in a governance gap. If it does, the process may be so poorly calibrated to what these models actually do that coverage becomes a formality.

Agentic AI compounds every challenge. Multi-step reasoning systems that autonomously execute tool calls and interact with external systems break SR 11-7's implicit assumption of periodic review with stable model form. Adversarial risks — prompt injection, jailbreak attacks, data poisoning — represent an entirely uncontemplated threat category.[^7]

> [!CAUTION]
> Third-party concentration around a handful of AI providers creates systemic risk that transcends traditional vendor management. Banks accessing foundation models through APIs cannot inspect model internals, cannot control version updates, and cannot perform traditional replication-based validation. The [FSB identified this concentration as a primary financial stability vulnerability in November 2024](https://www.fsb.org/2024/11/the-financial-stability-implications-of-artificial-intelligence/).

## A patchwork of responses, no unified framework

The regulatory response has been fragmented. In the U.S., no AI-specific MRM guidance has been issued. The OCC's [September 2025 Bulletin 2025-26](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html) described itself as "a first step" in a broader review. [Governor Barr's April 2025 speech](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/barr20250404a.htm) was explicit: regulators "should review and update existing standards on model risk management" — but this lacks the specificity of formal guidance. The FDIC added a different register in 2026 testimony to the House: working with the Fed and OCC to review the guidance, but with a stated goal of *loosening* it — a more tailored, risk-based approach for smaller institutions where, the agency argued, most models pose no material financial risk.[^8]

The [Treasury's Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0401) (February 2026) is the most thorough U.S. effort so far: 230 control objectives covering governance, data, model development, validation, and monitoring. It's voluntary but expected to shape examiner expectations. The [NIST AI RMF](https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-ai-rmf-10) (January 2023) and its [July 2024 Generative AI Profile](https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-generative-artificial-intelligence) provide structural foundation, but both are voluntary and cross-sectoral, lacking supervisory force.

The international contrast is sharp. The [UK PRA's SS1/23](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss) (May 2024) established five MRM principles with a broader model definition explicitly addressing qualitative outputs. [Canada's OSFI Guideline E-23](https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/guideline-e-23-model-risk-management-2027) (September 2025, effective May 2027) uses a technology-neutral definition covering AI/ML methods. The [EU AI Act](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng) classifies AI systems used for credit scoring as **high-risk** — imposing binding documentation, explainability, human oversight, and monitoring requirements, with compliance deadlines set for August 2, 2026.[^9]

Industry positions reveal a genuine divide. The [Bank Policy Institute](https://bpi.com/bpi-responds-to-white-house-office-of-science-and-technology-policys-rfi-regulatory-reform-on-artificial-intelligence/) called for outright repeal of SR 11-7 in April 2025, arguing the guidance discourages AI innovation. But senior model risk executives at [BPI's own member banks pushed back](https://www.risk.net/risk-management/7963135/bpi-says-sr-11-7-should-go-bank-model-risk-chiefs-say-%E2%80%98no%E2%80%99), fearing repeal would weaken risk management standards — a remarkable internal clash. The [American Bankers Association](https://www.aba.com/advocacy/policy-analysis/sfr-on-ai-innovation) called for updating — not repealing — the guidance, subject to notice and comment.

## Four scenarios for SR 11-7's future

The question is no longer whether SR 11-7 will be updated but how, when, and under what political conditions. My instinct is that the answer will be shaped less by intellectual clarity than by political timing and a triggering event nobody wants.

*Scenario 1: Incremental supplemental guidance.* Regulators issue FAQ documents or examination bulletins addressing AI within the existing SR 11-7 framework — analogous to [SR 21-8](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/SR2108.htm)'s extension of MRM principles to BSA/AML. This is the most probable near-term outcome. The fundamental definitional gaps remain unresolved.

*Scenario 2: Tiered modular update with AI-specific annexes.* New annexes address ML, generative AI, and agentic systems with differentiated requirements — expanded model definitions, continuous monitoring expectations, prompt engineering documentation standards. [McKinsey has proposed](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/risk-and-resilience/our-insights/derisking-machine-learning-and-artificial-intelligence) modifying 12 of SR 11-7's 25 existing risk elements and adding six new ones: interpretability, bias, feature engineering, hyperparameters, production readiness, and dynamic model calibration. The UK's SS1/23 and Canada's E-23 offer templates.

*Scenario 3: Full rewrite.* A new supervisory letter replaces SR 11-7 entirely, aligned with NIST AI RMF principles. This is the most intellectually coherent option but the least politically feasible. The current administration's deregulatory posture — including the [January 2025 revocation of Executive Order 14110 on AI safety](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/31/2025-02172/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence) — makes a major new regulatory framework unlikely before 2028.

*Scenario 4: Principles-based outcome supervision.* The regulatory approach shifts from prescriptive validation requirements toward outcome-based supervision, where banks demonstrate that AI systems produce fair, accurate, well-governed results without mandating specific validation methodologies. The risk is replicating the pre-crisis pattern of insufficient independent challenge. This "outcome-based" approach aligns more with [BPI's recommended changes](https://bpi.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/BPI-OSTP-AI-RFI-Response-10.27.25.pdf).

Open questions that will shape whichever scenario materializes:

- Who owns validation of a foundation model — the bank, the vendor, or a third-party auditor?
- How should banks document prompt engineering — as model configuration, system architecture, or a new category?[^10]
- How does a bank "retire" an LLM when the vendor controls versioning?
- What does human-in-the-loop really mean - is it a robust control that minimizes risk or a loosely defined "get-of-governance" loophole that is modern AI [regulatory arbitrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arbitrage#Regulatory_arbitrage)?

## The timeline is uncertain, but the direction is clear

SR 11-7's core insight — that models are "by definition imperfect representations of reality" and that managing model risk requires effective challenge by objective, informed parties with real influence — is more relevant in the AI era than it was in 2011. The challenge is not that the principles are wrong. It's that the institutional architecture for applying them must evolve.

Validation of a linear regression and validation of a multi-billion-parameter foundation model accessed through a third-party API are fundamentally different activities. The framework that solved the Gaussian copula problem is not the framework that will solve the prompt injection problem. But the philosophy — humility about models, independence in challenge, governance with teeth — is exactly what the AI era demands.

The question is whether regulators and industry can translate that philosophy into operational practice before the next model failure writes the next chapter.
